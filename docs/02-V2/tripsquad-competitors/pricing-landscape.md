## Value Metric Analysis

The market splits into three pricing paradigms:

1. **Per-user subscription** (Wanderlog, TripIt, Splitwise, Polarsteps) -- Standard SaaS. Each user decides independently. Problem: in groups, one person's refusal to pay fragments the experience.

2. **Free / ad-supported** (Google Maps, TripAdvisor, Tricount) -- Maximum adoption, but limited monetization and feature depth. TripAdvisor monetizes through booking affiliates.

3. **Organizer-pays** (TripSquad) -- Novel in this market. The organizer (trip planner) pays; everyone else is free. Eliminates group adoption friction but caps revenue at 1 paying user per group.

## Pricing Psychology Observations

- **Wanderlog** uses anchoring: generous free tier makes Pro feel like a bonus, not a necessity. The $4/month framing (vs $48/yr) is classic charm pricing.
- **TripIt** prices at $49/yr to signal "professional tool" -- aligned with business travel expense accounts.
- **Splitwise** at $49.99/yr is aggressive for an expense tracker; the free tier with ads drives upgrade through annoyance (ad-removal motivation).
- **Polarsteps** cleverly combines low subscription with high-margin physical products (photo books). The subscription is affordable; the real revenue is emotional purchases post-trip.
- **TripSquad at $39/yr** is well-positioned: below TripIt and Splitwise, psychologically under $40, and the organizer-pays model means the group perceives the tool as "free."

## Pricing Whitespace

1. **Group/team pricing** -- No competitor offers "one price covers the whole group" cleanly. TripSquad's model lives here alone.
2. **$20-40/yr for a comprehensive group tool** -- Most paid competitors are $48-50/yr per user. At $39/yr for the whole group, TripSquad is dramatically cheaper on a per-person basis (a group of 6 = $6.50/person/yr).
3. **Transactional/per-trip pricing** -- No competitor charges per trip. A $5/trip model could appeal to infrequent travelers.

The biggest "competitor" switching cost is **inertia**: groups already have a WhatsApp group and a Google Doc. The switching cost isn't technical -- it's behavioral.

## Red Flags

- If organizer-pays caps at $39/yr per group, TripSquad needs very high volume or additional revenue streams (booking affiliates, premium features, photo products) to build a venture-scale business
- Per-group-organizer LTV is significantly lower than per-user-seat models

## Yellow Flags

- No competitor has validated organizer-pays; this is both an opportunity and a risk
- Tricount's near-free pricing ($3/yr) in Europe sets low price expectations for group tools
